Today's Trend

Today leans heavily on correction-after-adaptation: MERIT for video-LLMs, GeoAlign for spatial MLLMs, and regional-adaptation work all argue that multimodal adaptation damages specific capabilities in predictable ways and that targeted recovery (merging, re-aligning, distilling) beats adaptation from scratch. Multilinguality is having a strong day too — three substantial papers (English is Not All You Need, Bridging Linguistic Gaps, ReasonXL) making the same point from different angles: English-centric training is a choice, not a default, and the choice is expensive. On data and benchmarks, MERRIN and SceneScribe-1M stand out, both pushing evaluation and training into messier, more realistic regimes. Generative-image work (GRN) is more speculative — interesting reframing of tokenization, unproven at scale. The long-video thread continues with practical token-budget engineering (Tempo, Mosaic) rather than architectural breakthroughs.

#1

English is Not All You Need: Systematically Exploring the Role of Multilinguality in LLM Post-Training

Mehak Dhaliwal, Shashwat Chaurasia, Yao Qin et al.

Mehak Dhaliwal, Shashwat Chaurasia, Yao Qin, Dezhi Hong, Thomas Butler

UC Santa Barbara; Amazon

TL;DR

Rare controlled ablation on multilingual SFT data at 8B with parallel translations isolating the coverage variable. Adding even one non-English language improves English; wider coverage disproportionately helps low-resource tongues.

Parallel-translation design says something cleaner about coverage vs. quantity than most "multilingual matters" papers. Whether it generalizes past 8B and past translated data is the open question.

A systematic, controlled study of the interplay between training language coverage, model scale, and task domain based on 220 SFT runs on parallel translated multilingual data mixtures spanning math reasoning and API calling, with models up to 8B. Increasing language coverage is largely beneficial across tasks and scales, with low-resource languages benefiting most; incorporating a single non-English language improves both English and cross-lingual generalization.

Reasoning Resides in Layers: Restoring Temporal Reasoning in Video-Language Models with Layer-Selective Merging

Zihang Fu, Haonan Wang, Jian Kang et al.

Zihang Fu, Haonan Wang, Jian Kang, Kenji Kawaguchi, Jiaying Wu

Affiliations not clearly listed (author list suggests NUS and collaborators)

TL;DR

Observes adaptation damages reasoning in specific layers, then recovers them by selectively merging from the text backbone — no training. Beats uniform merging and random layer selection.

Merging as a surgical diagnostic tool is a nice move. Whether "reasoning resides in layers" is durable or setup-specific is what to watch.

Multimodal adaptation gives LLMs perception but weakens reasoning — especially in video-LLMs where visual alignment impairs temporal reasoning. MERIT is a training-free, task-driven merging framework that searches layer-wise self-attention merging recipes between a video-LLM and its text-only backbone, improving temporal reasoning while preserving perception; generalizes across four benchmarks.

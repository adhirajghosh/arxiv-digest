Today's Trend

Two threads dominate today's submissions: (1) a maturing "science of benchmarking" push, with multiple papers directly targeting aggregate-score hiding, judge/temperature variance, and modality-conflict robustness in evaluation pipelines — a welcome correction to leaderboard-chasing culture. (2) Continued data- and recipe-centric VLM work, from EXAONE 4.5's bilingual industrial pipeline to lightweight self-supervised tuning boosts and block-diffusion retrofits. Multilinguality is showing up as both benchmarks (UNER v2) and targeted low-resource releases (AtlasOCR, ReasonXL for European languages), though low-resource coverage remains thin. Video and 3D are increasingly converging, with video-generation priors being repurposed for 3DGS restoration and video-LLMs being dissected for where reasoning lives. Absent today: big foundation-model announcements — the field seems to be pausing to measure itself.

Reasoning Resides in Layers: Restoring Temporal Reasoning in Video-Language Models with Layer-Selective Merging

Affiliations not clearly listed

Affiliations not clearly listed

Affiliations not clearly listed

TL;DR

Video-LLMs lose temporal reasoning during visual adaptation; selectively merging reasoning-critical layers from the base LLM with perception-critical layers of the video model recovers reasoning without sacrificing perception. A layer-wise mechanistic take on the perception↔reasoning tradeoff.

This framing — "reasoning resides in specific layers" — is the interesting claim and should generalize beyond video; the question is whether the "layer-critical" identification holds up under scaling and across diverse video benchmarks or is a curated win.

Multimodal adaptation equips large language models with perceptual capabilities but often weakens the reasoning ability inherited from language-only pretraining. This trade-off is especially pronounced in video-language models, where visual-temporal alignment impairs multi-hop temporal reasoning. The paper proposes a layer-selective merging procedure that fuses reasoning-critical layers from the pre-adaptation LLM with perception-critical layers from the adapted video-LM.

Affiliations not clearly listed

Affiliations not clearly listed

TL;DR

Uses a large paired video dataset (107.5K clips) and a video diffusion prior to systematically repair artifacts in 3D Gaussian Splatting reconstructions. Pairs a taxonomy of common 3DGS failure modes with a scalable restoration recipe.

A compelling bridge between generative video priors and 3D reconstruction — if the taxonomy is principled and the data scaling holds, this could become a standard tool. The risk is that video priors "hallucinate" geometry where 3DGS was simply under-observed.

ArtifactWorld is a framework for 3DGS artifact repair through systematic data expansion and a homogeneous dual-model paradigm. The paper establishes a fine-grained phenomenological taxonomy of 3DGS artifacts and constructs a training set of 107.5K diverse paired video clips, leveraging video generation priors to restore degraded Gaussian Splats.

Decomposing and Reducing Hidden Measurement Error in LLM Evaluation Pipelines

Affiliations not clearly listed

Affiliations not clearly listed

Affiliations not clearly listed

TL;DR

Treats LLM eval as a measurement problem and systematically decomposes its error sources — prompt wording, judge choice, decoding temperature, sample variance — then proposes variance-reducing estimators. Complements "Beyond Scores" nicely.

A long-overdue treatment. If the variance decomposition generalizes across benchmark families and the proposed estimators are cheap, this should be adopted widely; the risk is that the remedies are too specific to the experimental settings tested.

LLM evaluation scores carry hidden uncertainty: rephrasing prompts, switching judge models, or changing temperature can shift results enough to flip rankings. The paper decomposes the variance sources in LLM evaluation pipelines (prompt, judge, decoding, sampling) and proposes variance-reducing estimators that yield more reliable ranking conclusions.

TL;DR

A benchmark that stresses VLMs with deliberate visual-textual evidence conflicts to measure both "deflection" (over-reliance on one modality) and hallucination. Rare focused benchmark on the retrieval-vs-perception failure mode.

The failure mode is important and underexplored; benchmark quality will depend on whether the conflict scenarios are naturalistic or adversarial-contrived. If it's the latter, models will quickly overfit to the eval.

Large Vision-Language Models increasingly rely on retrieval to answer knowledge-intensive multimodal questions, but existing benchmarks overlook conflicts between visual and textual evidence.

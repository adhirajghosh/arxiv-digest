# arXiv Digest — April 17, 2026

## Top Highlights Today
- **English is Not All You Need** — 220-run controlled study showing even a single added non-English language during post-training lifts both English and cross-lingual performance.
- **MERIT / Reasoning Resides in Layers** — Training-free, layer-selective merging restores temporal reasoning in video-LLMs — clean diagnosis that visual alignment damages specific layers.
- **EXAONE 4.5** — LG's first open-weight VLM for industrial intelligence, a new data point alongside Qwen3-VL / InternVL.
- **MERRIN** — Multimodal retrieval + multi-hop reasoning over noisy, conflicting web evidence.
- **Generative Refinement Networks** — Hierarchical Binary Quantization attacks the tokenization bottleneck in visual synthesis.

## Papers

### 1. English is Not All You Need: Systematically Exploring the Role of Multilinguality in LLM Post-Training
**Authors:** Mehak Dhaliwal, Shashwat Chaurasia, Yao Qin, Dezhi Hong, Thomas Butler
**Affiliations:** UC Santa Barbara; Amazon
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13286
**Abstract:** A systematic, controlled study of the interplay between training language coverage, model scale, and task domain based on 220 SFT runs on parallel translated multilingual data mixtures spanning math reasoning and API calling, with models up to 8B. Increasing language coverage is largely beneficial across tasks and scales, with low-resource languages benefiting most; incorporating a single non-English language improves both English and cross-lingual generalization.
**TL;DR:** Rare controlled ablation on multilingual SFT data at 8B with parallel translations isolating the coverage variable. Adding even one non-English language improves English; wider coverage disproportionately helps low-resource tongues.
**Take:** Parallel-translation design says something cleaner about coverage vs. quantity than most "multilingual matters" papers. Whether it generalizes past 8B and past translated data is the open question.

### 2. Bridging Linguistic Gaps: Cross-Lingual Mapping in Pre-Training and Dataset for Enhanced Multilingual LLM Performance
**Authors:** Not clearly listed in search data
**Affiliations:** Affiliations not clearly listed
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.10590
**Abstract:** Multilingual LLMs struggle with cross-lingual tasks due to data imbalances and monolingual bias in pre-training. Introduces a Cross-Lingual Mapping Task during pre-training that enhances cross-lingual alignment without compromising monolingual fluency, plus a supporting dataset.
**TL;DR:** Auxiliary cross-lingual mapping objective at pre-training time (not post-training), targeting the high/low-resource gap where it is baked in.
**Take:** Pre-training-stage interventions are under-explored because they are expensive. If ablations are honest about scale, this is more interesting than another SFT paper.

### 3. Reasoning Resides in Layers: Restoring Temporal Reasoning in Video-Language Models with Layer-Selective Merging
**Authors:** Zihang Fu, Haonan Wang, Jian Kang, Kenji Kawaguchi, Jiaying Wu
**Affiliations:** Affiliations not clearly listed (author list suggests NUS and collaborators)
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.11399
**Abstract:** Multimodal adaptation gives LLMs perception but weakens reasoning — especially in video-LLMs where visual alignment impairs temporal reasoning. MERIT is a training-free, task-driven merging framework that searches layer-wise self-attention merging recipes between a video-LLM and its text-only backbone, improving temporal reasoning while preserving perception; generalizes across four benchmarks.
**TL;DR:** Observes adaptation damages reasoning in specific layers, then recovers them by selectively merging from the text backbone — no training. Beats uniform merging and random layer selection.
**Take:** Merging as a surgical diagnostic tool is a nice move. Whether "reasoning resides in layers" is durable or setup-specific is what to watch.

### 5. Generative Refinement Networks for Visual Synthesis
**Authors:** Not clearly listed in search data
**Affiliations:** Affiliations not clearly listed
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13030
**Abstract:** Introduces GRNs, a visual synthesis paradigm addressing diffusion inefficiency via Hierarchical Binary Quantization (HBQ), reframing synthesis as iterative refinement over structured binary codes.
**TL;DR:** HBQ as a replacement for continuous-latent diffusion and VQ-style tokenizers, with iterative refinement. Targets the many-step diffusion sampling cost.
**Take:** "Next-generation beats diffusion" claims rarely survive compute-matched scaling. Watch the ablations.

**Affiliations:** Affiliations not clearly listed

**Abstract:** Tempo adapts MLLMs for hour-long videos by using a small VLM as a local temporal compressor before a larger reasoning model.

## Overall Trend Today
Today leans heavily on correction-after-adaptation: MERIT for video-LLMs, GeoAlign for spatial MLLMs, and regional-adaptation work all argue that multimodal adaptation damages specific capabilities in predictable ways and that targeted recovery (merging, re-aligning, distilling) beats adaptation from scratch. Multilinguality is having a strong day too — three substantial papers (English is Not All You Need, Bridging Linguistic Gaps, ReasonXL) making the same point from different angles: English-centric training is a choice, not a default, and the choice is expensive. On data and benchmarks, MERRIN and SceneScribe-1M stand out, both pushing evaluation and training into messier, more realistic regimes. Generative-image work (GRN) is more speculative — interesting reframing of tokenization, unproven at scale. The long-video thread continues with practical token-budget engineering (Tempo, Mosaic) rather than architectural breakthroughs.
